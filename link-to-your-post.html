An Introduction to RNA-seq

RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) is a powerful technology that has revolutionized the field of transcriptomics. It allows researchers to quantify and analyze the RNA molecules present in a biological sample at a given moment, providing a comprehensive snapshot of gene expression. This capability is crucial for understanding a wide range of biological processes, from disease progression to cellular differentiation.

How Does RNA-seq Work?

The RNA-seq workflow can be broken down into a few key steps:

  1. RNA Isolation: First, RNA is extracted from a cell or tissue sample.
  2. cDNA Synthesis: The fragile RNA molecules are converted into more stable complementary DNA (cDNA).
  3. Library Preparation: The cDNA is fragmented, and adapters are added to prepare it for sequencing. This is a critical step that ensures the fragments can be read by the sequencing machine.
  4. Sequencing: The prepared cDNA library is loaded into a high-throughput sequencer, which generates millions of short reads.
  5. Data Analysis: The raw sequencing reads are mapped to a reference genome, and bioinformatic pipelines are used to quantify gene expression, identify novel transcripts, and detect gene fusions or splice variants.

Key Applications of RNA-seq

RNA-seq has a broad range of applications across biological research and medicine:

  • Gene Expression Profiling: It can be used to compare gene expression between different conditions, such as healthy vs. diseased tissue, or treated vs. untreated cells.
  • Novel Transcript Discovery: RNA-seq can reveal the existence of previously unknown genes or isoforms.
  • Fusion Gene Detection: In cancer research, RNA-seq is a powerful tool for identifying gene fusions that can drive tumor growth.
  • Biomarker Discovery: By identifying genes that are differentially expressed, researchers can find potential biomarkers for disease diagnosis or prognosis.
